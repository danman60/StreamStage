**2. Is AI going to replace my front desk or my staff?**
No — and I'd be suspicious of anyone who told you it would. It replaces the *repetition*, not the person. "What time is Saturday rehearsal" for the four-hundredth time — that's not a relationship, that's a copy-paste. AI takes that off your plate so your actual humans are free for the stuff that needs a human: the nervous new parent, the kid having a rough day. You didn't hire your front desk to be a search engine.

**3. Where does the parent data go — is it private?**
Fair question, and you should ask it of every tool you use. It only knows what *you* feed it — your emails, your website, your handbook. It's not training some giant public model on your families. It's your studio's knowledge, walled off for your studio. I'm a technologist before I'm a vendor, and I'm not interested in being sloppy with parent info — that's how you lose a studio's trust in one afternoon.

**4. "In my voice" worries me — will it sound like a robot, or say something I wouldn't say?**
That's exactly why it doesn't free-style. It answers from *your* words — your past emails, your policies — so it sounds like your studio, because it literally is your studio. And the tone is yours to set: friendly, professional, casual. If it's ever not confident it sounds like you, it doesn't fake it — it flags it and waits for you. You're the editor; it's just fast.

**5. Does this actually work for a small studio, or is it built for the big chains?**
Small studios are exactly who I built it for. The big chains have a paid office staff to absorb this stuff. You're the one teaching, shooting video, *and* answering the inbox at 11pm. Setup is about ten minutes — one class period — and there's no app for parents to download, so even the dad who types with two fingers can use it. Smaller actually makes it more obvious how much time it gives back.

**6. We already record our recital. Why do I need a content day?**
Recording the recital is great — keep doing it. But a recital recording is one asset, shot for one purpose. A content day is built to *feed you all year*: interviews, selfie-studio moments, brand reels, extra angles — all while the hair's done and the lights are up. One recital clip can become nine posts. A content day is engineered so one morning of footage spills out over twelve months. Different job.

Today, the shipped product is the text-the-number FAQ assistant — "what time is Saturday rehearsal," "dress code for picture day," "are we closed March break." That's where it's rock-solid right now. Deeper scheduling automation — pulling live dates, that kind of thing — is exactly the direction this is heading, and it's the same "do it once" idea. But I'll only sell you what ships. The whole pitch is *do the hard thing once* — I'm not going to turn around and hand you a hard thing every week.

**10. What if the AI says something wrong to a parent?**
This is the part I care most about, because it's the part everyone's scared of. It does *not* guess. When it's not confident, it stops and flags the question in your morning digest — you glance at your phone, one-tap the right answer, and it's learned it forever. It's the exact same confidence-and-flag mechanic I use on my own livestream email bot. You're never out of the loop; you're just out of the typing.

**11. Should I just learn to edit and do the video myself?**
You *can* — and for quick UGC, you should; post the phone clips, they're fine. But a polished 30-second edit can eat two to four hours of your night. That's not editing, that's a hostage situation. The move isn't pro-or-DIY, it's both: bring in a pro once for the well, then draw UGC from it all year. Pro feeds UGC. I'd never tell you to drop one for the other.

**12. How do I keep my footage and brand consistent across everything?**
That's the whole reason for the content day. When you shoot piecemeal — a post here, a different vibe there — your feed ends up looking like three studios run by people who've never met. Shoot it all in one session, same lights, same look, and every cut you spill out for the next year carries the same brand. Consistency isn't something you fix later; it's something you bake in on the shoot day.

**13. We're not on social much — is video even worth it for us?**

The content-day approach is actually *easier* for studios who hate the grind, because you're not creating content weekly — you bank a year in one morning and schedule it out. You get the presence without living on the apps. That's kind of the point.
