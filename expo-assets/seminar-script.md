## SEGMENT 1 — THE CONTENT DAY (~8 min)

**[INTERACT — open it]** Before I tell you anything: how are you making video right now? Shout it out. Phone in the lobby? Hired someone for the gala? Niece with a ring light? *(Take 3–4 answers, react to each by name. This seeds the room and tells you which examples to lean on.)*

**The problem.** Most studios do content the exhausting way: shoot a thing, edit the thing, post the thing. Then do it again tomorrow. Shoot, edit, shoot, edit — it'll kill you. And because it's piecemeal, it *looks* piecemeal: one post over here, the next one a totally different vibe, and now your brand looks like three different studios run by three people who've never met.

**The fix — the content day.** Pick one day. Get everybody in. Hair done, makeup on, costumes on. Rent some lights, maybe a hazer — and yes, warn the building first, because nothing derails a content day like a fog machine introducing itself to the smoke alarm. You get all the elements that make it *cook*, and you shoot it ALL at once. Then you take your time spilling out the edits over the next twelve months.

Think of it as stations. One four-hour shoot, multiple setups running:
- **Interview / story station** — owner and dancers talking. This is the heart. People doing people things — which, by the way, is the one thing AI can't generate for you. Nobody's prompting your recital into existence.
- **Selfie studio** — fun, fast, dancers make their own shareable moments while you shoot something else.
- **Routines / movement** — the polished stuff, the brand-reel material.
- **Extra angles** — pro habit: every device is just another focal length. A drone is a focal length. A GoPro on a dancer's wrist mid-turn is a focal length. *(aside)* On the drone — get good outside first. I've turned a couple of drones into very expensive wall art learning to fly indoors. The difference is I get to expense mine. You crash one down a hallway of seven-year-olds, that's a whole different meeting.

**[LIVE EXAMPLE]** — *hold up phone / point to screen:* here's a single recital clip, and here are nine posts I cut from just that one clip. Same footage. Nine.

The rule of the day: **maximize assets while the hair is done and the lights are up.** Two cameras is literally twice the minutes from the same setup. The more you grab while everyone's ready, the more mileage you get all year.

**Pro vs UGC — you need both.** Full disclosure: I'm a video vendor. So I am, technically, contractually obligated by my own mortgage to stand here and tell you pro video is great. *(beat)* It is, though. But I'd never tell you to drop UGC because you've got pro, or skip pro because you've got UGC. UGC on its own is maybe a two-out-of-ten — and that's fine, post it. The magic is the *in-between*: bring in a pro, and that pro footage becomes the well you draw from all year — you cut verticals from it, caption it, remix it. **Pro feeds UGC.**

And the editing math is real: you *can* learn to edit. But a 30-second video can eat two to four hours of your night. That's not editing, that's a hostage situation. From one content day, a pro hands you a year's worth. That's the trade.

**What you walk out with.** A homepage story video. Background video that makes your website feel alive. Recital and competition reels. And dozens of short social cuts you schedule out over months. Same brand, every post, all year.

**[DEMO]** — roll a real content-day footage reel here (30–45 sec).

**Land it:** One morning in. Twelve months of posts out.

## SEGMENT 2 — AI / AUTOMATION (~8 min)

Quick honesty: everything in my world keeps circling back to AI right now, and I don't say this lightly — this is as big a shift as the internet was. It really is. The good news for a studio owner is it's finally pointed at the boring stuff, the stuff you actually want gone.

**[INTERACT — the pain-point round, the heart of the talk]** So forget video for a second. I'm a technologist, so I want to know: what's the menial, repetitive thing in your week that, if it just *did itself*, would give you hours back? Shout them out. *(Take answers. Repeat each one back so the room hears it. The transcript proves these come every time:)*
- **"Parent emails."** — "Can you make AI answer them for me?" Yes. That's literally the next two minutes.
- **"The monthly newsletter."** — Are we still sitting down writing those by hand?
- **"Rehearsal scheduling."** — Staring at a wall of dates and teacher availability.
- **"The lobby TV."** — Ugly, you update it every month, you hate it.

*Hold whatever they say — you'll point back at it. Now pay off the open loop.*

**Pay off the loop first.** Remember the email I answered without typing, mid-livestream? Here's what happened. I'm running a competition — eight camera feeds, a tracking camera, an operator yelling in my ear — and a parent emails: "I never got the link." Now... they got the link. They did. I checked. They did, though. But they're stressed, and I cannot stop. So I'd built a little email spider. It reads the message, checks my calendar for which event I'm at *that day*, finds the right stream link, drafts the reply, scores its own confidence — "I'm 90% sure this is the answer" — fires it, and pings me so I see it went. Parent helped in seconds. I never touched my keyboard. I was, at the time, busy.

**That's the whole AI half — I built it for my own pain first.** And your version is sitting in your inbox right now: "What time is Saturday rehearsal?" "Classes during March break?" "Dress code for picture day?" You answer those a hundred times a year. Directors lose ten-plus hours a week to repeat parent questions. You did not open a dance studio to be a receptionist. *(callback: "...you opened it to do the thing on the sign.")*

**Pull, don't push.** Here's why blasting *more* info doesn't fix it. You email it, you text it, it's on the BAND app, it's on the website — and parents are still buried. My wife runs her own business, she's sharp, and on recital day she is texting the studio owner a question whose answer is — I promise you — sitting in her own inbox. I've watched it happen. I live with it. It's not carelessness; being *pushed* info means it's in a pile somewhere. People want to **pull**: ask one place, get the answer, now. That's the move.

**So that's what we productized — StudioSage.** The AI front desk for your dance studio. Parents **text a number.** No app, no login — if they can text, they can use it, which means even the dad who still types with two fingers is covered. And it doesn't make things up. It answers from **your** stuff.

**[LIVE ANIMATED DEMO — the KB builds in front of them]** Watch this. Right now this studio's knowledge base is empty. *(screen: empty KB.)* I forward in a batch of real parent emails — *(emails fly in, cards populate: "Saturday rehearsal 9am," "March break = closed," "picture day dress code…")* — connect the website, drop in the handbook PDF. *(counter ticks up.)* Thirty seconds ago it knew nothing. Now a parent texts "what time is Saturday rehearsal?" and — *(live reply appears)* — there it is, in the studio's own words. It didn't get smart in general. It got smart about *your* studio. Setup is about ten minutes. One class period, and you've trained your front desk.

**Same human-in-the-loop you just saw on the livestream.** When it's not confident, it does NOT guess. It flags the question in a **morning digest** — you glance at your phone, one-tap the answer, and it learns it forever. Exact same confidence-and-flag mechanic. You're never out of the loop. You're just out of the *repetition*.

**[DEMO — get them texting]** Pull out your phones. Text this number right now, ask it anything a parent would. *(Let real questions hit the live screen. Show one land instantly, and if one flags, even better — "see, it didn't fake it.")*
