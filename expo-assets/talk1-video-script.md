## OPEN (2 min)

Quick show of hands. Who here shot, edited, and posted a video for your studio in the last week? Keep them up if you did it again the week before. And the week before that. Right — keep those hands up, because I want you to notice something: somewhere along the way, the job quietly became *content creator* on top of, you know — teaching dance. The thing on the sign. Congratulations, you have at least two jobs and you're getting paid for one of them.

And if you're not posting, you start to feel like you don't exist — so you're scrolling at 11pm watching everyone else's studio post, with a thousand other things to do. Great hobby. Terrible business plan.

I'm a videographer. I film studios, recitals, competitions — this is what I do all year. And today is one idea: **do the hard thing once, and let it pay you back all year.** I'm going to show you how to capture a year of video in a single morning — and exactly what to do with it so your studio looks like ONE studio, every post, for twelve months.

## SEGMENT — THE PROBLEM (~4 min)

**[INTERACT — open it]** Before I tell you anything: how are you making video right now? Shout it out. Phone in the lobby? Hired someone for the gala? Niece with a ring light? *(Take 3–4 answers, react to each by name. This seeds the room and tells you which examples to lean on.)*

**The exhausting way.** Most studios do content the exhausting way: shoot a thing, edit the thing, post the thing. Then do it again tomorrow. Shoot, edit, shoot, edit — it'll kill you. And because it's piecemeal, it *looks* piecemeal: one post over here, the next one a totally different vibe, and now your brand looks like three different studios run by three people who've never met.

---

## SEGMENT — THE CONTENT DAY (~9 min)

**The fix — the content day.** Pick one day. Get everybody in. Hair done, makeup on, costumes on. Rent some lights, maybe a hazer — and yes, warn the building first, because nothing derails a content day like a fog machine introducing itself to the smoke alarm. You get all the elements that make it *cook*, and you shoot it ALL at once. Then you take your time spilling out the edits over the next twelve months.

Think of it as stations. One four-hour shoot, multiple setups running:
- **Interview / story station** — owner and dancers talking. This is the heart. People doing people things — which, by the way, is the one thing AI can't generate for you. Nobody's prompting your recital into existence.
- **Selfie studio** — fun, fast, dancers make their own shareable moments while you shoot something else.
- **Routines / movement** — the polished stuff, the brand-reel material.
- **Extra angles** — pro habit: every device is just another focal length. A drone is a focal length. A GoPro on a dancer's wrist mid-turn is a focal length. *(aside)* On the drone — get good outside first. I've turned a couple of drones into very expensive wall art learning to fly indoors. The difference is I get to expense mine. You crash one down a hallway of seven-year-olds, that's a whole different meeting.

**[LIVE EXAMPLE]** — *hold up phone / point to screen:* here's a single recital clip, and here are nine posts I cut from just that one clip. Same footage. Nine.

The rule of the day: **maximize assets while the hair is done and the lights are up.** Two cameras is literally twice the minutes from the same setup. The more you grab while everyone's ready, the more mileage you get all year.

**Pro vs UGC — you need both.** Full disclosure: I'm a video vendor. So I am, technically, contractually obligated by my own mortgage to stand here and tell you pro video is great. *(beat)* It is, though. But I'd never tell you to drop UGC because you've got pro, or skip pro because you've got UGC. UGC on its own is maybe a two-out-of-ten — and that's fine, post it. The magic is the *in-between*: bring in a pro, and that pro footage becomes the well you draw from all year — you cut verticals from it, caption it, remix it. **Pro feeds UGC.**

And the editing math is real: you *can* learn to edit. But a 30-second video can eat two to four hours of your night. That's not editing, that's a hostage situation. From one content day, a pro hands you a year's worth. That's the trade.

**What you walk out with.** A homepage story video. Background video that makes your website feel alive. Recital and competition reels. And dozens of short social cuts you schedule out over months. Same brand, every post, all year.
