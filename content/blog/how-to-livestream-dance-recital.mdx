## Why livestream your recital?

Watching a recital in person is magical, but not everyone can make it to the theatre. Families travel, grandparents live across the country, and sometimes health or weather get in the way. A **livestream** lets you share your dancers’ hard work with far‑flung relatives and preserves the performance for later re‑watching. It also raises your studio’s profile: parents see that you invest in high‑quality experiences and prospective students can see your culture without stepping in the door.

Livestreaming a recital, however, is not as simple as pointing a phone at the stage and pressing “go live.” Poor audio, dropped frames and bad camera angles will frustrate your audience. To deliver a professional broadcast you need careful planning, appropriate equipment and contingency plans. In this guide you’ll learn how to build a reliable setup and decide when to call in a production team like **StreamStage Media**.

## Plan your stream before opening night

A recital involves dozens of dances, costume changes and cue sheets, so you should treat your livestream like a mini‑television production. Professional streamers emphasise the importance of having a run of show, checking gear and rehearsing with any guests. Map out the timing of each routine, note where you’ll need wide shots versus close‑ups and decide who is responsible for switching cameras. Planning also includes licensing: if you use commercially recorded music you need the right permissions to sync those songs to video. The National Federation of State High School Associations explains that streaming video requires both a **master license** (permission to use a particular recording) and a **synchronization license** that grants you the right to adapt the song to a video.

Before the big day:

- **Create a run of show.** Include each routine, approximate durations and any narration or awards.
- **Check your gear early.** Set up cameras, cables and microphones a week before to ensure everything works. A test run into a private group or “record only” mode will reveal audio or framing issues.
- **Test your internet connection.** EventLive’s streaming guide recommends at least 3 Mbps upload speed for a stable broadcast. Run a speed test at the venue and, if possible, hard‑wire into the router.
- **Schedule interviews and guest checks.** If you plan to include a host or teacher announcements, schedule a brief call to check their camera and sound.

## Gather the right equipment

### Cameras and capture

Your cameras are the audience’s eyes. Built‑in laptop webcams can work in a pinch, but they limit your framing and look amateurish. External webcams or camcorders offer better optics, adjustable fields of view and superior low‑light performance. Many studios use a **multi‑camera setup** for recitals. This might involve a wide static shot from the back of the theatre and one or two operated cameras closer to the stage. According to a professional streaming guide, multi‑camera production requires matching different devices (webcams, DSLRs or camcorders), ensuring they output a compatible signal, and connecting them via capture cards or HDMI splitters. Plan your camera positions in advance so you can capture solos, group formations and quick transitions without frantically panning.

If your cameras don’t connect via USB, you’ll need **capture cards**. These devices convert HDMI or SDI outputs into a USB signal your computer can ingest. For multiple cameras, invest in a USB hub with sufficient bandwidth or an external switcher that outputs a single feed to your streaming software. Always mount your cameras on sturdy tripods; a shaky feed distracts viewers. For handheld shots (for example, tracking a dancer onto the stage), a gimbal stabiliser keeps movement smooth.

### Audio: the deal‑breaker

Audience members will forgive an occasional blurry frame, but they will abandon a stream with poor sound. The Ecamm article on live streaming mistakes stresses that audio is the most important aspect of a broadcast. Place a microphone close to the stage or tap into the theatre’s soundboard so music and dialogue are captured cleanly. A directional shotgun mic on a boom stand reduces ambient audience noise, while a stereo pair can convey the richness of live accompaniment. Always monitor audio levels and wear headphones; if the feed clips or distorts, adjust your gain. Wireless lavalier microphones are useful if you have hosts or presenters moving around the stage. Before showtime, perform a full sound check and ensure your audio remains in sync with the video feed.

### Lights and power

House lighting seldom flatters video. Dark corners and coloured stage washes can cause your cameras to hunt for focus. Add supplementary lighting where permitted—softboxes near your operated cameras or LED panels directed at the stage—to even out exposure. Keep cables tidy and away from traffic areas. Bring extension cords, surge protectors and a backup power supply; nothing halts a stream faster than a dead battery.

### Computer and software

You need a computer with sufficient processing power to handle multiple video inputs and encoding. A modern laptop with a multi‑core processor and 16 GB of RAM is ideal. Install a dedicated streaming application such as **OBS Studio** or your preferred production software. OBS allows you to create separate scenes for different camera angles, add overlays and graphics, and configure transitions. Assign hotkeys so you can cut between cameras quickly. Set your output resolution (1080p is standard for recitals) and bitrate; adjust these based on your available upload speed. If the stream stutters or drops frames, reduce the bitrate until the feed stabilises.

## Mastering multi‑camera magic

One camera from the back of the auditorium can’t capture the nuance of a recital. Multi‑camera production adds depth—wide shots show formations, side angles reveal group dynamics and close‑ups capture emotion. Setting up multiple cameras requires more than just plugging them in. The EventLive guide suggests adding each camera as a separate source in OBS, creating scenes for different combinations and setting up transitions. Here’s how to keep your switching seamless:

1. **Define roles.** Assign one person to operate each movable camera and another to manage switching. Communicating quietly via headsets helps coordinate movement.
2. **Label your scenes.** In OBS, create scenes like “Wide Shot,” “Stage Left Close‑Up” and “Split View.” Practice switching between them with hotkeys.
3. **Match your cameras.** Adjust white balance and exposure on each camera so cuts look consistent.
4. **Use a multi‑view monitor.** A second display showing all camera feeds allows the switcher to anticipate action.
5. **Plan cut points.** During rehearsals, note when solos begin or formations change so you can plan your switches. Avoid rapid switching; let each shot breathe.

## Choosing a streaming platform

Once your hardware is ready, decide where your audience will watch. Popular platforms include YouTube Live, Vimeo and private services. Consider:

- **Privacy controls.** If you want to limit viewership to families, use a platform with password protection or unlisted links.
- **Monetisation.** Some studios sell digital tickets or monetize their streams. Choose a service that allows pay‑per‑view or integrates with your registration system.
- **Playback quality.** Platforms optimise streams differently; test how your feed looks on various devices and networks.
- **Audience interaction.** Chat features allow parents to congratulate dancers in real time. Encourage viewers to send messages and respond when possible. Ecamm’s experts note that engagement is a cornerstone of live streaming—responding to comments, hosting live Q&As and giving shout‑outs keeps viewers invested.

## Practice, troubleshoot and build redundancy

No matter how much planning you do, live streams can go wrong. A cardinal rule in streaming is to never go live without testing everything. Perform a full dress rehearsal: set up your equipment at the venue, connect to your streaming platform and record the run. Watch the playback for audio‑video sync issues and check whether your upload speed holds steady. Identify any dead spots for wireless signals and adjust your camera positions accordingly.

Prepare for contingencies:

- **Backup internet.** Have a mobile hotspot ready. If your primary connection fails mid‑stream, switch to the hotspot.
- **Redundant recordings.** Record the program locally on your computer or an external recorder; if your stream drops, you can still deliver a full video to families.
- **Spare equipment.** Bring extra cables, batteries and microphones; small failures can derail a stream.
- **Backup operator.** If someone gets sick, have another staff member trained on the basics of switching and troubleshooting.
