## Why recital videography pricing varies so much

If you have ever requested quotes from multiple videographers for your studio's year-end recital, you know the frustration: one company quotes $800, another quotes $4,000, and a third asks how many dancers you have before giving any number at all. The range is enormous because the scope of work can vary just as dramatically. A single-camera hobbyist shooting from the back row delivers a fundamentally different product than a multi-camera production team with dedicated audio, live switching, and individual dancer edits.

Understanding what drives the cost helps you make an informed decision, set accurate parent expectations, and even turn your recital media into a **revenue stream** for your studio. In this guide we break down the most common pricing models, share StreamStage's actual per-dancer rates, and walk through real examples so you can see exactly where your money goes.

## Common pricing models for recital videography

### Flat rate

A videographer quotes a single price for the entire event regardless of how many routines or dancers are involved. Flat-rate pricing is simple and predictable, but it often favours smaller recitals. A studio with 200 dancers pays the same as one with 40, which means the per-dancer cost can swing wildly. Studios also have no way to pass the cost along proportionally to families, so the expense sits entirely on the studio's budget.

### Per-video or per-routine

Some companies charge by the number of routines they record and edit. This model scales more naturally with the size of your show, but it can create awkward incentives. Studios may feel pressure to limit the number of dances or combine groups to reduce cost. It also does not account for routines with very different numbers of performers: a solo and a 30-person finale might cost the same per-video even though the production effort differs.

### Per-dancer

A per-dancer model ties the cost directly to the number of participants. Every family pays the same media fee regardless of how many routines their dancer appears in, and the studio's total cost scales proportionally with enrolment. This is the model StreamStage uses because it aligns the interests of the studio, the families, and the production team. Studios can set a parent-facing media fee that covers the production cost and generates additional revenue.

## The revenue model: how studios profit from recital media

The most compelling aspect of per-dancer pricing is that it creates a **revenue opportunity** for your studio. Here is how it works: StreamStage charges you a per-dancer rate. You charge each family a media fee as part of their recital registration. The difference between what you charge and what you pay is yours to keep. There is no revenue share, no percentage split, and no hidden platform fee.

### Example 1: Video only, 100 dancers

A medium-sized studio with 100 dancers books multi-camera videography at $25 per dancer.

- **Studio's cost to StreamStage:** 100 dancers x $25 = **$2,500**
- **Studio charges parents:** $30 per dancer media fee
- **Studio's revenue from parents:** 100 x $30 = **$3,000**
- **Studio's profit:** $3,000 - $2,500 = **$500**

That $500 covers part of your venue rental, contributes to costume subsidies, or simply adds to your bottom line. The parents pay a reasonable $30 for a professionally produced multi-camera video of every routine their child appears in, which is far less than hiring their own videographer or buying a DVD from a traditional recital video company.

### Example 2: Full bundle, 150 dancers

A larger studio with 150 dancers books the full bundle (video, livestream, and photography) at $22 per dancer.

- **Studio's cost to StreamStage:** 150 dancers x $22 = **$3,300**
- **Studio charges parents:** $40 per dancer media fee
- **Studio's revenue from parents:** 150 x $40 = **$6,000**
- **Studio's profit:** $6,000 - $3,300 = **$2,700**

At $40 per dancer, families receive professionally shot and edited video of every routine, a livestream link they can share with out-of-town relatives, and a gallery of edited photos. That is exceptional value for the price, and the studio walks away with $2,700 in profit from a service that would have cost them money under a traditional flat-rate model.

## How per-dancer pricing compares to flat rate

Studios often ask whether a flat-rate quote from a local videographer is a better deal. The answer depends on the size of your recital and the quality of the deliverables, but there are structural advantages to per-dancer pricing worth considering.

**Scalability.** A flat rate that seems affordable for a 50-dancer recital becomes a bargain at 200 dancers, but most flat-rate videographers adjust their price upward for larger events anyway. Per-dancer pricing scales linearly and predictably.

**Revenue potential.** Flat-rate pricing leaves no room for the studio to generate revenue unless you mark up the videographer's price to parents, which feels awkward when there is a single lump sum. Per-dancer pricing naturally integrates into your registration fee structure.

**Accountability.** When a videographer is paid per dancer, every family is a stakeholder. The production team is motivated to deliver quality for each participant because the studio's willingness to rebook depends on parent satisfaction.

**Transparency.** Parents understand "your media fee is $35 per dancer" far more intuitively than "the studio paid $3,500 for a videographer and we're splitting the cost." Per-dancer pricing feels fair and straightforward.

## What to budget for your recital

If you are planning your first professionally produced recital, here is a rough framework for budgeting:

- **Video only:** Budget $25 per dancer for production costs and plan to charge parents $30 to $35 for a modest profit margin.
- **Video and livestream:** Budget $30 to $35 per dancer (using bundle pricing where available) and charge parents $40 to $45.
- **Full bundle (video, livestream, and photography):** Budget $22 to $35 per dancer depending on studio size and charge parents $40 to $50.

The exact numbers depend on your studio's size, your market, and what parents in your community expect to pay. Studios in metropolitan areas often charge higher media fees without pushback because parents compare the cost to hiring their own photographer or videographer, which would cost far more for inferior results.
