## One camera tells only part of the story

Dance is a visual art that unfolds across space. A single, fixed camera can capture choreography from a safe distance, but it misses the magic of a close‑up on a dancer’s face, the excitement of a formation change or the intimacy of a duet. Viewers at home quickly tire of static wide shots. Professional broadcasts mix multiple angles to create a sense of presence, rhythm and emotion. For recital livestreams and competitions, a **multi‑camera setup** transforms your broadcast from a documentation into an experience.

This approach is not just about adding more cameras. It’s about choosing the right angles, synchronising audio, rehearsing camera moves and switching shots at the right moment. It demands careful planning and technical know‑how, but the payoff is a polished, engaging stream. In this article you’ll learn why one camera isn’t enough, how to design a multi‑camera system, and how to manage the complexity so your dancers shine.

## The limitations of a single camera

Many studios start with a single camera at the back of the auditorium. It captures the whole stage but flattens depth, hides facial expressions and makes solos feel distant. Without variety, viewers tune out. The EventLive guide notes that single cameras offer improved video quality over webcams but still leave you with a static view. A wide shot cannot show intricate footwork or spotlight a dancer entering from stage left. When you need to highlight award presentations or backstage interviews, a single camera can’t pivot fast enough.

A single camera also forces you to compromise on composition. You must choose whether to frame for formations or individuals and hope that everything stays inside the frame. Lighting changes across the stage may cause exposure issues, and dancers moving toward the wings can exit the shot entirely. Viewers who paid for a livestream expect more than a locked‑off view.

## Why multi‑camera elevates your stream

Adding cameras introduces dynamism, variety and professionalism. EventLive’s guide explains that a multi‑camera setup allows you to mix wide and close‑up angles, create smooth transitions and offer viewers a TV‑like feel. The pros include:

- **Dynamic storytelling.** Close‑ups capture emotion, medium shots show formations and wide shots provide context. Switching among them keeps viewers engaged.
- **Flexibility.** If a dancer misses their mark, you can cut to another angle. When a soloist performs centre stage, a dedicated camera can follow them without disrupting the wide view.
- **Professional polish.** Multi‑camera streams resemble televised performances, conveying credibility and value. This professionalism reflects well on your studio or competition.

There are trade‑offs. Multi‑camera setups are more expensive and require more people to operate. They introduce complexity in syncing video and audio and matching colour and exposure across devices. However, the investment pays dividends in audience satisfaction and potential revenue from digital tickets.

## Designing your camera layout

Every venue is different, but a few principles apply when mapping your camera positions:

1. **The master wide.** Place one camera at the back or centre of the house to capture the full stage. This shot is your safety net—it ensures you never miss a movement even when other cameras reposition.
2. **Side angles.** Position cameras on stage left and stage right to add depth and capture diagonal movement. These cameras can provide medium shots and reveal formations that are hidden from the front.
3. **Close‑up/roaming camera.** Use a shoulder‑mounted camera or gimbal to follow soloists or zoom in on duets. This angle brings viewers onto the stage and creates intimacy. Remember to keep movements smooth and rehearsed.
4. **Backstage or audience camera (optional).** For award announcements or candid moments, a backstage camera adds variety. Use it sparingly to avoid distracting from the performance.

Plan your shots during rehearsals. Note when you’ll want to zoom in and when to hold a wide shot. Communication among camera operators and the person switching is essential. Provide operators with a run sheet so they know which dancer to follow and when to prepare for a transition.

## Matching and syncing your cameras

Different cameras produce different colours and exposures. To avoid jarring cuts, match settings across devices: adjust white balance, shutter speed and ISO so shots look consistent. Use manual focus to prevent autofocus hunting when dancers move across the frame. If you mix camera brands, calibrate them using a grey card and test under stage lighting.

All cameras must be synchronised with the same audio source. If each camera records its own audio, you risk lag between angles. Feed audio from your mixing board into your switcher or streaming software. Use headphones to monitor sync. In a pinch, you can use a hand clap at the start of the performance to align tracks, but a single, central audio feed is more reliable.

## Switching and directing the show

The art of multi‑camera streaming lies in switching. Using software like **OBS Studio**, assign each camera to a source and create scenes for different views. EventLive recommends using scene transitions and hotkeys to switch smoothly. A multi‑view monitor showing all feeds lets you anticipate action.

Here are tips to direct your livestream:

- **Rehearse with your team.** Run through the recital with your camera operators so you all know when to switch. Ecamm’s streaming guide emphasises the importance of rehearsing and testing gear in advance.
- **Call shots clearly.** The director (switcher) should cue operators using discreet comms. For example, “Camera 2 ready… take 2.” This keeps transitions clean.
- **Let shots breathe.** Avoid rapid cutting; hold each shot long enough for viewers to take in the action. Only switch when there’s a clear change in choreography or emotion.
- **Use transitions intentionally.** Hard cuts work for most switches. Use fades or wipes sparingly for effect, such as moving from a backstage introduction to the stage.
- **Monitor audio levels.** The switcher should watch audio meters and ensure levels are consistent across angles. Sudden changes in volume distract viewers.

## Dealing with the logistics

Multi‑camera setups require more cables, capture cards and technical expertise than single‑camera systems. You may need multiple USB inputs or an external hardware switcher to handle several HDMI feeds. Label your cables and secure them to avoid trip hazards. Budget extra time for setup—arriving at the venue the day before is often necessary.

Additionally, consider:

- **Crew size.** You’ll need at least one operator per mobile camera and one person to switch. Someone should also monitor the audio mix.
- **Communication.** Use headsets or a clear line of sight so your team can coordinate silently.
- **Redundancy.** Bring spare cables, extra batteries and a backup camera. If one camera fails, you can still deliver a multi‑angle feed.
- **Space constraints.** Ensure your camera locations don’t block audience sight lines or emergency exits. Obtain venue permission for equipment placement.

## When to invest in professional help

For studios running occasional recitals or competitions with modest budgets, starting with two or three cameras and volunteer operators is feasible. But if you’re producing large competitions or high‑stakes recitals, consider hiring a professional crew. The pros bring broadcast switchers, colour‑matched cameras, wireless communication systems and experienced directors who know how to tell your story. They also handle complex tasks like real‑time graphics, lower thirds and replay. Outsourcing your multi‑camera production frees you to focus on teaching and staging.
