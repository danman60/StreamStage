## Different videos serve different goals

A single promo video is powerful, but it can’t tell every story. Prospective students want to see your teaching style, parents want to know what classes feel like and alumni love reliving recital highlights. By diversifying your video library, you engage audiences at different stages of their relationship with your studio. Below are five essential video types, along with tips on creating them and the complexities each one introduces.

## 1. The brand or promo reel

Your **promo reel** is your flagship. It condenses your values, atmosphere and services into a one‑minute clip that compels viewers to book a class. As we discussed in our promo‑video guide, quality is paramount: shaky footage and poor lighting send the wrong message. Keep it under 60–90 seconds because viewers quickly lose interest after the first minute. Use a mix of wide shots, mid‑shots and close‑ups to tell a complete story and film frequently to gather enough material.

Complexity arises in planning your storyline and licensing music. You’ll need to secure master and synchronization licenses for any commercial tracks, or choose royalty‑free music that reflects your style. A professional production team can help storyboard, license music and edit the footage into a cohesive narrative. See our detailed guide on [creating a promo video](/blog/dance-studio-promo-video-guide) for more.

## 2. Class preview videos

Families often hesitate to register for a new class because they don’t know what to expect. **Class previews** are short clips (30–60 seconds) that show a typical warm‑up, a combination and the teacher’s style. They’re especially helpful during enrollment season when you’re launching new styles or age groups. To shoot a preview:

- Plan your camera angles so you capture the instructor’s cues and the dancers’ responses. A single wide shot might be enough, but adding a second camera for close‑ups shows details like footwork or facial expressions.
- Keep the edit concise—viewers watching on social media have short attention spans.
- Include a call to action at the end (e.g., “Register now,” “First class free”).

The challenge with class previews is scheduling filming during a real class without disrupting instruction. Have parents sign release forms and film on a day when attendance is high. Avoid peak enrollment periods when teachers are busy answering questions.

## 3. Student spotlights

**Spotlight videos** celebrate individual dancers. They highlight progress, passion and personality, and they’re powerful tools for retention because they make families feel valued. A spotlight might feature interviews with a student and their parents, clips from rehearsal and performances, and B‑roll of them warming up or laughing with friends.

When filming spotlights:

- Use an external microphone for interviews to capture clear audio.
- Keep the runtime under three minutes; even dedicated audiences have limited time.
- Address privacy: obtain written consent and avoid sharing sensitive details.

The complexity comes in storytelling. You must weave footage and interviews into a narrative that feels authentic rather than promotional. Consider hiring a professional interviewer to draw out genuine responses and an editor to craft a compelling arc.

## 4. Recital and competition highlights

Nothing generates excitement like a highlight reel from your recital or competition. These videos remind current students why they love your studio and show prospects the end result of your training. To create a **recital highlight**:

- Capture multi‑camera coverage: wide shots for formations, tight shots for solos and audience reactions.
- Sync your cameras’ white balance and exposure so the shots match when editing.
- Choose energetic music and ensure you have the appropriate licenses.

Highlight edits can be time‑consuming. Sorting through several hours of footage to find the best moments requires patience and editorial judgment. A professional editor will also ensure that transitions align with music and that the pace feels exciting without overwhelming the viewer.

## 5. Parent or student testimonials

Word‑of‑mouth is still one of the most effective marketing tools, and **testimonial videos** capture it on camera. A good testimonial features a parent or student speaking candidly about why they love your studio. Tututix cautions against overly scripted testimonials because they come across as fake. Encourage interviewees to tell personal stories—how your studio built their confidence, helped them make friends or prepared them for auditions.

For testimonials:

- Choose a quiet location and use a lapel or shotgun mic for clean sound.
- Frame the subject at eye level with soft lighting; avoid distracting backgrounds.
- Keep the video under two minutes and insert B‑roll of the speaker’s child dancing to illustrate their points.

Editing testimonials involves cutting pauses and repetitions while preserving authenticity. Add lower‑third graphics to identify speakers and end with a subtle call to action.

## Building your library over time

You don’t need all five videos tomorrow. Start with one or two and add new types as your budget and schedule allow. Shoot often—even simple smartphone clips of class combos or group photos can evolve into more polished pieces later. The key is to capture the breadth of your studio: the hard work in class, the camaraderie in the lobby, the exhilaration on stage.
