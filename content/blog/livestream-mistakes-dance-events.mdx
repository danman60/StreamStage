## The harsh reality of live video

You’ve built an entire season around your dancers’ big moment. Parents, friends and alumni can’t wait to watch online. Then the stream stutters, the audio crackles, and the whole production feels amateur. Nothing ruins the magic faster than technical problems you could have avoided. In this post we’ll unpack seven mistakes that derail dance livestreams and show you how to plan around them.

Streaming live is a complex, moving target. It isn’t just pressing “Go Live” on a phone; it’s juggling bandwidth, cameras, audio, graphics and contingencies. Professional broadcasters rehearse every segment and test their gear. Dance events present additional challenges: quick costume changes, unpredictable run times and emotional stakes. The good news is that most failures are preventable when you know where the pitfalls lurk.

## 1. Trusting unreliable internet

Livestreaming requires stable upload speed and low latency. Many studios assume their home Wi‑Fi is enough, only to discover that their upstream connection can’t handle HD video. EventLive notes that a good stream needs at least **3 Mbps of upload bandwidth** and cautions that wireless connections are prone to drops. Ecamm’s live‑streaming experts recommend always connecting via **wired Ethernet** and running a speed test well in advance.

Problems often start when other devices compete for the network. If parents are uploading backstage photos or the front‑desk computer is syncing files, your stream could buffer or freeze. Always

- Use a dedicated wired connection for your broadcast computer.
- Test your upload speed on the day of the event and reduce your stream bitrate if the speed is low.
- Have a backup connection, such as a 5G hotspot, and know how to switch mid‑stream.

## 2. Neglecting audio

Audiences will forgive a slightly soft video, but they tune out instantly when they can’t hear the music or dancers. Ecamm emphasises that **audio is the most important part of a broadcast**; invest in quality microphones, record in a quiet space and do a sound check. Many recital livestreams rely on the camera’s built‑in mic, capturing echoey room sound and crowd chatter. Instead:

- Feed the soundboard’s mix directly into your streaming computer to get clean music and voice.
- Place ambient microphones near the stage to capture applause and atmosphere.
- Use headphones while monitoring to catch hiss, clipping or unexpected noises.

Streaming multiple stages or competitions adds complexity. You may need to balance voice‑over introductions, emcee commentary and music cues. Create separate audio channels and assign a volunteer or technician to monitor levels throughout the show. The difference between harsh, distorted audio and a balanced mix can make or break your stream’s professionalism.

## 3. Forgetting to plan the show

Dance recitals have dozens of quick transitions; routines sometimes change order at the last minute. Without a run‑of‑show document you’ll miss cues and cut away mid‑dance. Ecamm notes that professional streamers outline every segment, check gear and rehearse ahead of time. Your run‑of‑show should include:

1. **Cue sheet** – List each routine, performer names, song length and special notes (e.g., solos, acrobatics).
2. **Camera plan** – Decide when to use wide shots, when to punch in on solos and when to show audience reactions. Multi‑camera switching adds dynamism, but only if someone is tasked with calling shots.
3. **Timing** – Note expected durations and build in buffer for costume changes. Share this plan with stage managers so they can signal if a routine runs long or starts early.

Without planning, volunteers may point a camera at an empty stage or miss a critical lift. StreamStage Media’s multi‑camera directors create shot lists and rehearse transitions before the audience arrives.

## 4. Skipping the rehearsal and gear check

Perhaps the most preventable mistake is going live without testing. Ecamm warns that not testing your equipment is a major cause of livestream failures. Even if you’ve streamed before, variables like lighting, room acoustics and new hardware can surprise you. A proper rehearsal should include:

- **Dry run** – Stream privately or to a closed Facebook group. Watch on another device to confirm audio/video quality and latency.
- **Backup gear** – Bring extra cables, batteries and a spare camera. If a cable fails mid‑performance, you need to swap it without disrupting the show.
- **Power management** – Plug cameras and encoders into uninterruptible power supplies. Recitals often trip breakers when stage lighting and sound equipment draw from the same circuits.

Doing a run‑through also uncovers on‑stage issues: Are there dark spots the lighting rig doesn’t cover? Does the marley floor reflect light into the lens? It’s far easier to reposition a camera during a rehearsal than during a live performance.

## 5. Using the wrong camera angles

One static camera at the back of the theatre rarely does justice to dance. Viewers miss facial expressions, footwork and formation changes. EventLive highlights that **multi‑camera setups** create a more dynamic, professional look, but they require careful matching and switching. Common mistakes include:

- **Too wide all the time** – A wide shot captures formations but loses detail. Mix wide shots with close‑ups of solos and group details.
- **Too close all the time** – A close shot on a soloist can cut off other dancers. When groups are large, use a medium or wide angle.
- **Crooked or obstructed shots** – Tripods should be level, and camera operators should avoid shooting through audience heads. Place cameras on risers or aisles and mark no‑standing zones.

If you can’t manage multiple camera operators, at least add one additional angle: a static wide shot for safety and a roaming operator for feature shots. ## 6. Failing to engage or promote your stream

Even the best production falls flat if no one knows about it or if viewers feel ignored. Ecamm points out that many streamers neglect to promote their broadcast; you should schedule your stream, share links and remind families about it in advance. During the event, keep the virtual audience engaged with chat and interactive elements.

Promotion tips:

- Create a registration page on your website where families can purchase access (password‑protected if needed) and sign up for reminders.
- Send links via email and text several days before the show and again the morning of the performance.
- Share highlights on social media with behind‑the‑scenes photos or rehearsal teasers.

During the live show, acknowledge chat messages, display encouraging comments on screen and use polls or Q&A segments during intermission. Engagement fosters community and increases the chances that families will share your stream with relatives.

## 7. Overlooking licensing and legalities

Many studio owners ignore copyright until a platform mutes their audio or removes their video after the fact. The **National Federation of State High School Associations** explains that streaming performances with commercial music requires a **master license** for the specific recording and a **synchronization license** to pair the music with video. Without these permissions you could face takedowns or fines. Be proactive:

- Use royalty‑free music or work with a licensing agency to secure the rights for your recital songs.
- Include licensing fees in recital budgets and communicate the importance to parents.
- Keep records of your licenses to respond quickly to copyright claims.

Dance competitions often have their own blanket licenses, but livestreaming is not always covered. Check with the competition organizer or licensing bodies before streaming categories that use popular songs.
